## Summary
Cubyz's player-engagement design principles: how the player should feel, depth in simplicity, curiosity, multiple solutions, explaining when needed, micro moments, and making the world feel alive.

## Explanation
"How would the player feel?": put yourself in the shoes of a player encountering a mechanic for the first time and ask this. The goal is to make the player feel how you want them to, whether that be satisfaction, fear, or intrigue.

Depth in Simplicity: keep the game approachable to new players, so appearing simple is key. However, what keeps a player around is the game's depth -- design mechanics around being simple on the surface, but able to be expanded upon, upgraded, or customized to meet the player's needs.

Fuel the Player's Curiosity: fill the world with secrets that will keep the player interested and engaged with exploration.

Problems have Multiple Solutions: problems faced in a particular age of progression should have multiple solutions, instead of just one solution for everything. Example: in the "Pre-Caves Age," the player lacks coal (which spawns low and in darkness) -- solutions include finding a dim above-ground light source, searching for a cave with exposed coal, wandering into darkness to find a bright-enough cave, finding an above-ground structure that grants coal, or digging straight down. Having these options prevents the player from getting stuck on a seemingly insurmountable barrier.

Explain when Needed: progress-related mechanics need explanations or subtle hints so players don't get stuck, but should stay simple on the surface, leaving out details the player can find through intuition.

Embrace Micro Moments: tiny things the player does between larger events -- travelling, parkour, mining, building, crafting, managing inventory, fighting. These moments largely influence the player's mood, so making them satisfying and consistent is a must.

Make the World Feel Alive: to add immersion, creatures should perform behaviors outside of player input, such as hunting, playing, migrating, eating, or sleeping.
