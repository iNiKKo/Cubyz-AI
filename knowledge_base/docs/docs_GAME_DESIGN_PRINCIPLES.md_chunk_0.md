## Summary
This document outlines game design principles for Cubyz, focusing on progression, break the cycle, player engagement, and balancing.

## Explanation
Cubyz is a voxel sandbox game with an infinite world that focuses on great adventures and immersive exploration. Players are given freedom to choose their own path through the world. Progression in Cubyz encourages interaction with various mechanics, such as exploring, building, crafting, and strategizing. Specific examples of progression include: Explore to Build (the player needs resources from exploration for building), Survive to Explore (caves and structures may have enemies and traps that must be faced to explore safely), Strategize to Survive (preparation with tools, weapons, and gear is necessary for survival during trips), and Build to Strategize (defenses are needed for events, invasions, and bosses; workstations need upgrading for better gear). The 'Break the Cycle' design rules aim to enhance player engagement by discouraging certain behaviors: No Dimensions (separate places fit into Cubyz's world physically rather than being split into dimensions), No Teleportation (it diminishes immersion and exploration), No Automation (quick, infinite resources discourage actual exploration), Mobs Don't Respawn Naturally (clearing dangerous areas makes them safe to build/explore and prevents mob farming), No Passive Animals (animals defend themselves or flee, forcing the player to strategize for hunting), No Unbreakable Tools (attachment to one tool discourages making others and using low-tier materials). Avoid Clutter involves finding uses for existing items before adding new ones and preventing unwanted items from entering inventory. Use Realism Tastefully means Cubyz is whimsical with its own rules, inspired by the real world but not copied without a good reason. Avoid Excessive Additions requires genuine functional reasons for variants or additions; try modifying existing content first. Function over Form emphasizes considering a mechanic's function before finalizing its design/theme to avoid pointlessness. Player engagement principles include 'How would the player feel?' (putting oneself in the shoes of a player encountering a mechanic for the first time), Depth in Simplicity (keeping the game approachable while providing depth through customization and expansion), Fueling Curiosity (filling the world with secrets that keep players engaged), Problems have Multiple Solutions (providing multiple solutions to problems faced at different progression stages, such as finding coal before exploring caves), Explain when Needed (giving progress-related mechanics simple explanations or hints without revealing all details upfront), Embrace Micro Moments (valuing small actions like traveling, parkour, mining, building, crafting, managing inventory, fighting, and interacting with the world to influence player mood positively). Balancing involves keeping players vulnerable at all times through 2OP4ME (Players need to be vulnerable at all times to avoid power imbalances), Trade-offs (if a player is given something beneficial, it should come with an appropriate drawback or trade-off). Little details include Big Trees vs Small Trees (big trees are for building/decoration while small trees can be chopped down), Vegetation fitting biome climates (Toadstools prefer humid areas and Boletes prefer nutritious areas), and Caves being creepy and mysterious (the music gets scarier, monsters become more vicious, and cave generation becomes precarious as the player descends).
